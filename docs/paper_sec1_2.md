# Closing the Rounding Gap in Differentiable Constraint Programming with Augmented Lagrangian Dual Updates

## 1. Introduction

Sinkhorn-based differentiable solvers for AllDifferent constraints converge reliably in the continuous domain. Across every tested problem size — $N = 8$ through $N = 512$ — the continuous loss reaches exactly 0.000000. The doubly stochastic matrix $P \in DS_N$ produced by Sinkhorn normalization satisfies all row-sum and column-sum constraints to numerical precision. The problem appears at discretization. Hungarian rounding of the converged continuous solution produces integer assignments that violate hard diagonal constraints. At $N = 32$, rounding fails in 1 of 3 trials. From $N \geq 64$ onward, it fails universally: 6 diagonal violations at $N = 64$, 8 at $N = 128$, and worse beyond. Zero continuous loss does not imply a feasible discrete solution. The gap between the continuous optimum and the nearest feasible integer assignment — the *rounding gap* — persists at every scale where it appears, and closing it is the central question of this work.

The rounding gap matters because differentiable programming for combinatorial optimization enables gradient-based solvers on GPU hardware. This is a fundamentally parallel computation model. Sequential constraint programming solvers — CP-SAT [Perron & Furnon 2023], Gecode, OR-Tools — exploit clause learning, propagation, and large neighborhood search, but they do not exploit data parallelism across thousands of arithmetic units. If the rounding gap closes reliably, differentiable CP becomes a viable parallel alternative for problem families where continuous relaxations carry structural information about feasible solutions. The broader differentiable combinatorial optimization literature demonstrates the potential: Erdos Goes Neural [Karalias & Loukas 2020] learns graph combinatorial surrogates end-to-end, Gumbel-Sinkhorn networks [Mena et al. 2018a] produce differentiable permutation samples, DiffSAT extends differentiable relaxation to satisfiability, and DiffILO applies it to integer linear optimization. All of these methods face discretization as the final bottleneck.

Prior approaches do not close the rounding gap. Gumbel-Sinkhorn sampling [Mena et al. 2018a] adds stochastic diversity to the rounding step by injecting Gumbel noise into the log-assignment matrix before Sinkhorn normalization. The method treats the rounding problem as a sampling problem: generate many perturbed candidates, round each one, keep the best. At $N = 64$, 500 Gumbel samples all fail — every perturbation of the continuous optimum rounds to a permutation that violates at least one diagonal constraint. The problem is not a lack of diversity in rounding candidates. The problem is the *location* of the continuous optimum itself. It sits in a region of the Birkhoff polytope where all nearby vertices (permutation matrices) are infeasible. Fixed-penalty methods can increase the constraint weight $\lambda$ to push the optimum elsewhere, but large fixed penalties degrade the conditioning of the optimization landscape and slow inner-loop convergence. The fundamental issue is that the continuous objective is blind to which specific constraints will be violated after rounding, and a fixed penalty cannot adapt to the violation pattern of a particular problem instance.

Augmented Lagrangian methods [Bertsekas 1999; Nocedal & Wright 2006] address exactly this limitation. ALM maintains per-constraint dual variables $\mu_k$ that accumulate based on observed violations. After each inner optimization, the update rule applies: $\mu_k \leftarrow \mu_k + \rho \cdot g_k(x^*)$, where $g_k(x^*)$ measures the violation of constraint $k$ at the current solution. Constraints that remain violated after rounding receive growing Lagrange multipliers. Constraints that are satisfied receive no additional pressure. The dual variables steer the continuous optimum toward a region of the Birkhoff polytope where Hungarian rounding succeeds. Two to three outer iterations suffice at all tested scales ($N \in \{64, 128, 256, 512\}$). The dual variables learn problem-specific structure: diagonal constraints of different lengths have different violation propensities, and the ALM update weights them accordingly without any prior knowledge of the problem geometry.

The contributions of this paper are four. First, an empirical diagnosis of the rounding gap establishes that it is a function of problem size $N$, is not closed by Gumbel-Sinkhorn sampling (500 samples, all infeasible at $N = 64$), and grows sublinearly with $N$. Second, an ALM formulation with per-diagonal dual variables closes the gap: 0 violations at $N = 64$ (2.5 s), $N = 128$ (28 s), $N = 256$ (176 s), and $N = 512$ via hybrid ALM + greedy repair (34 min). Third, a GPU crossover analysis on Apple MPS hardware quantifies when batched Sinkhorn iterations become faster than CPU execution: crossover at $N = 16$, peak speedup of $4.95\times$ at $N = 64$. Fourth, a warm-start pathway — supplying the continuous solution as a hint to CP-SAT — produces feasible solutions in 100% of trials across all tested sizes, including $N = 64$ and $N = 128$ where standalone rounding fails.


## 2. Background and Related Work

**Sinkhorn normalization and the Birkhoff polytope.** Sinkhorn [1964] proved that any square matrix with strictly positive entries converges to a doubly stochastic matrix under alternating row and column normalizations. Birkhoff [1946] established that the set $DS_N$ of $N \times N$ doubly stochastic matrices is the convex hull of the $N!$ permutation matrices — the Birkhoff polytope. Together these results provide the geometric foundation for differentiable constraint programming: Sinkhorn iteration projects an arbitrary log-assignment matrix onto the continuous relaxation of the set of feasible integer assignments. The iteration operates as follows: given a matrix $A$, set $S_0 = \exp(A / \tau)$ for temperature $\tau > 0$, then alternate row normalization $S_{t+1/2} = D_r^{-1} S_t$ and column normalization $S_{t+1} = S_{t+1/2} D_c^{-1}$, where $D_r$ and $D_c$ are diagonal matrices of row and column sums respectively. Each iteration costs $O(N^2)$ and consists entirely of matrix operations — element-wise division, matrix-vector products, and broadcasting — all of which map directly to GPU execution.

**Differentiable sorting and ranking.** Adams and Zemel [2011] first applied Sinkhorn normalization to ranking problems, showing that a differentiable relaxation of the permutation operator enables gradient-based learning-to-rank. Cuturi [2013] connected Sinkhorn iterations to entropy-regularized optimal transport, establishing a dual interpretation: the Sinkhorn distance between two distributions can be computed by Sinkhorn iterations on the cost matrix, and the regularization parameter $\epsilon$ plays the same role as the temperature $\tau$. Mena et al. [2018a] introduced the Gumbel-Sinkhorn network, which adds Gumbel noise to the log-assignment matrix before Sinkhorn normalization to produce stochastic, differentiable permutation samples — the rounding strategy evaluated and found insufficient in this paper. Mena et al. [2018b] applied Sinkhorn networks to learning permutations end-to-end in supervised settings. These papers establish the direction of differentiable permutation operators. The present work identifies where this direction breaks down: at the rounding boundary between continuous and discrete solutions.

**Differentiable combinatorial optimization.** Karalias and Loukas [2020] proposed Erdos Goes Neural, a framework that trains a graph neural network to minimize a differentiable surrogate of a combinatorial objective (maximum independent set, minimum dominating set, graph coloring). The surrogate loss reaches near-zero in the continuous domain; the final step is rounding to a discrete solution. DiffSAT extends differentiable relaxation to Boolean satisfiability, and DiffILO applies similar principles to integer linear optimization. These methods share a common architecture: relax the discrete domain, optimize the continuous surrogate with gradient descent, round the result. All of them face the rounding gap — the continuous surrogate can be satisfied while the discrete solution is infeasible — and none address it through adaptive dual-variable updates. The standard remedies are either larger fixed penalties (which degrade conditioning) or repeated sampling (which fails when the continuous optimum lies in an infeasible region of the relaxation polytope).

**Augmented Lagrangian methods.** The Augmented Lagrangian Method was introduced independently by Hestenes [1969] and Powell [1969]. The method augments a constrained optimization problem $\min f(x) \text{ s.t. } g(x) \leq 0$ by adding both a linear dual term and a quadratic penalty term to the objective: $\mathcal{L}(x, \mu, \rho) = f(x) + \mu^T g(x) + (\rho/2) \|g^+(x)\|^2$, where $g^+(x) = \max(0, g(x))$ denotes the constraint violation. The dual update $\mu \leftarrow \mu + \rho \cdot g^+(x^*)$ after each inner minimization converts the history of constraint violations into a steering signal for subsequent iterations. Bertsekas [1999, Chapter 4] provides the convergence theory: under mild regularity conditions, ALM converges to a KKT point without requiring the penalty parameter $\rho$ to grow unboundedly. Nocedal and Wright [2006, Chapter 17] give the numerical analysis perspective, emphasizing that ALM maintains good conditioning because the dual term carries the accumulated violation pressure — the quadratic term $\rho$ serves as a stabilizer, not as the primary enforcement mechanism. This distinguishes ALM from pure penalty methods, where $\rho$ must grow large to enforce constraints, destroying the conditioning of the inner problem. The present work applies ALM in a batched, GPU-native formulation where each of $B$ parallel restarts carries its own vector of dual variables, one per diagonal constraint.

**Constraint programming and CP-SAT.** CP-SAT [Perron & Furnon 2023] is the reference modern constraint programming solver. It combines clause-driven conflict learning, constraint propagation, large neighborhood search, and LP relaxations into a single solver that handles Boolean, integer, and interval variables. On $N$-Queens — the benchmark problem used throughout this paper — CP-SAT solves $N = 64$ in 89 ms and $N = 128$ in 463 ms (single-threaded, median of 3 trials). DiffCP does not compete with CP-SAT on raw sequential speed at any scale tested. The value proposition is different: GPU scalability for the Sinkhorn inner loop (which achieves $4.95\times$ speedup at $N = 64$ on Apple MPS) and the warm-start pathway, where the continuous solution provides an integer hint that CP-SAT accepts and uses to seed its search. The Hungarian algorithm [Kuhn 1955] — the rounding step that converts a doubly stochastic matrix to a permutation — runs in $O(N^3)$ on CPU after each inner optimization. For large $N$, a batched GPU implementation of Hungarian rounding would be beneficial but is not yet implemented in this work.

No prior work combines batched Sinkhorn optimization over $B$ parallel restarts, Augmented Lagrangian dual updates per restart with per-constraint granularity, GPU-native execution on Apple MPS or NVIDIA CUDA, and a warm-start pathway to a production CP solver — each component exists independently; the combination and the empirical diagnosis of the rounding gap are the contributions of this paper.
